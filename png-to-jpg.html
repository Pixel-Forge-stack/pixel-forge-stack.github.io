PNG vs JPG

What Is PNG and Why Convert It to JPG?

PNG (Portable Network Graphics) is a lossless image format. It stores every pixel exactly as-is, supports full transparency, and never loses detail no matter how many times you re-save it. That precision comes at a cost, though — PNG files can be five to twenty times larger than an equivalent photo saved as JPG, because nothing about the image is thrown away to save space.

JPG (JPEG) takes the opposite trade-off. It uses lossy compression, intelligently discarding the tiny details the human eye barely notices, in exchange for dramatically smaller files. A PNG screenshot or photo that weighs 8 MB can often become a 400–800 KB JPG with no visible difference at normal viewing size.

Converting PNG to JPG is almost always about one thing: making the file smaller and easier to share, upload, or store, without needing pixel-perfect precision or transparency anymore.

Quick way to think about it: PNG is for editing, layering, and anything needing transparency. JPG is for the final, finished photo you actually want to send, post, or publish.
Why Convert

The Real Reasons People Convert PNG to JPG

1. Dramatically Smaller File Size

This is the single biggest reason to convert. A batch of PNG screenshots or exported photos can quickly eat through storage and slow down uploads. JPG compression routinely cuts file size by 80–95% for photographic content, which matters enormously for:

  • Email attachments with strict size limits
  • Uploading to websites where large images slow down page load (and hurt SEO)
  • Sharing dozens or hundreds of images without hitting storage limits
  • Mobile data usage when uploading or sending images

2. Universal, Frictionless Compatibility

JPG is the default format nearly every platform, printer, and piece of software expects for photographs. While PNG is supported almost everywhere too, some older software, print services, and bulk-upload systems specifically ask for JPG, and using it avoids compatibility surprises.

3. Removing Transparency on Purpose

If your PNG has a transparent background and you need a clean, solid background instead — for example, preparing a photo for a printed document or a platform that doesn't support transparency — converting to JPG automatically fills the transparent areas with a solid color (typically white), giving you a predictable, ready-to-use image.

4. Faster Websites and Better SEO

Large, uncompressed PNGs are one of the most common causes of slow-loading web pages. Search engines factor page speed into rankings, so swapping heavy PNG photos for optimized JPGs on a website can genuinely improve both load time and search visibility.

5. Simplifying Photo Libraries

Photos exported as PNG from screenshot tools, design software, or certain cameras take up unnecessary space when the transparency and lossless precision were never actually needed. Converting them to JPG in bulk reclaims storage without a noticeable quality difference.

Heads Up

What Happens to Transparency When You Convert?

PNG transparency has no equivalent in JPG — the JPG format simply doesn't have a way to store "no color" for a pixel. When you convert a transparent PNG to JPG, every transparent pixel is automatically filled with a solid background color, almost always white, so the final image looks the way it would if you'd placed it on a white page.

If you need to keep transparency, don't convert to JPG at all — keep the file as PNG, or convert to another format that supports transparency such as WEBP. Converting to JPG should be a deliberate choice made once you're sure you no longer need to see through any part of the image.

Quality

Understanding JPG Quality After Conversion

JPG compression works by discarding data that's least noticeable to the human eye, particularly in busy, photographic textures like skin, sky, water, and foliage. For most photos, a well-converted JPG looks visually identical to the PNG original at normal viewing distances and typical screen sizes.

Where it's more visible is in images with flat colors, sharp lines, or small text — screenshots, diagrams, and logos can show slight blurring or soft "ringing" around hard edges after conversion. If your PNG falls into that category and you're planning to zoom in or print it large, it's worth comparing the JPG output closely before replacing the original.

Good habit: always keep your original PNG. Treat the JPG as the final, shareable export rather than the file you continue editing, since every additional JPG save recompresses the image and slowly reduces quality further.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Will converting PNG to JPG reduce image quality? ▾
JPG uses lossy compression, so a small amount of fine detail is discarded during conversion. For photographic images, this loss is usually invisible to the eye at normal viewing size. For screenshots, text, or flat-color graphics, slight softness can appear around sharp edges — always keep your original PNG in case you need pixel-perfect quality later.
What happens to transparent areas in my PNG? ▾
They get filled with a solid background color, almost always white, because JPG cannot store transparency at all. If you still need a see-through background, don't convert to JPG — keep the PNG or use WEBP instead.
Why is my JPG so much smaller than the original PNG? ▾
That's the entire point of the format. PNG stores every pixel losslessly; JPG compresses the image by removing data the eye won't easily notice. A reduction of 80–95% in file size is completely normal and expected.
Can I convert several PNG files at once? ▾
Yes. You can drop multiple images onto the tool and each is converted independently and privately, entirely inside your browser.
Is there a limit on image size or resolution? ▾

Does this work on mobile devices? ▾
Yes. Since conversion happens in the browser itself rather than on a remote server, it behaves identically on desktop, tablet, and phone browsers.
Is my PNG file uploaded to a server? ▾
No. The entire conversion happens locally on your device using your browser's built-in Canvas engine. Nothing is transmitted, stored, or accessible to anyone else.
Do I need an account or subscription to use this? ▾

Can I also convert other formats to JPG here? ▾
Yes. Along with PNG, this tool also accepts WEBP, GIF, and BMP files and converts all of them to JPG using the same one-click process.
Should I keep both the PNG and the JPG? ▾
Generally, yes. Keep the PNG as your editable master copy (especially if it has transparency or needs future edits), and treat the JPG as the compressed, shareable version you actually send or publish.
