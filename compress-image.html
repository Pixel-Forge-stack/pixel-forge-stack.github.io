Why It Matters

Why Should You Compress Your Images?

Image file size has a bigger impact on your daily digital life than most people realise. Here are the main reasons why compressing images is so important.

Websites load faster. Images are usually the largest files on any web page. A single uncompressed photo can easily be 5MB or more. When someone visits your website, their browser has to download every image on the page. The bigger those images are, the longer the page takes to load. Studies have shown that a one-second delay in page load time can reduce conversions by up to 7%. Google also uses page speed as a ranking factor, which means slow-loading images can hurt your position in search results.

You save storage space. If you have hundreds or thousands of photos on your phone or computer, compressing them can free up significant storage space without making any visible difference to the images themselves.

Sharing is faster. Sending a 10MB photo over WhatsApp, email, or any other messaging app takes much longer and uses more mobile data than sending a 500KB version of the same photo. Compressing images before sharing them saves time and data for both you and the person you are sending to.

You stay within upload limits. Many platforms have file size limits for uploads. Email services often cap attachments at 10MB or 25MB. Government portals and job application forms sometimes require photos to be under 100KB or 200KB. Pixel Forge Stack helps you hit those targets quickly and easily.

Pro Tips

Tips for Getting the Best Compression Results

80% quality is the sweet spot for most images. At 80% quality, most people cannot see any difference compared to the original, but the file size is typically 60–70% smaller. This is the setting we recommend as the starting point for most use cases.

Use WEBP for the best results. WEBP is a modern image format developed by Google that produces smaller file sizes than JPG at the same visual quality. If you are using images on a website, WEBP is the best choice. All modern browsers support it.

For photos, use JPG or WEBP. For graphics with text, use PNG. JPG compression works very well for photographs but can create visible artefacts around sharp edges and text. If your image contains a lot of text or line art, PNG will give better results.

Resize before compressing for even smaller files. If you have a 4000x3000 pixel photo that you are only going to display at 800x600, use our resize tool first to bring it down to 800x600, then compress it. Combining resizing and compression gives you the smallest possible file size.

Check the before and after sizes. Our comparison bar shows you the original size and the compressed size in real time. Keep an eye on it as you move the quality slider to find the best balance between file size and visual quality for your specific image.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Will compressing an image make it look worse? ▾
At quality settings above 70–80%, most people cannot see any visual difference between the original and the compressed image. The compression reduces file size by removing data that the human eye cannot easily perceive. Below 50% quality, you may start to notice some blurring or artefacts, especially around edges and fine details.
Is my image uploaded to a server? ▾
No. Your image is never uploaded to any server. The entire compression process happens inside your browser on your own device. No one else can see or access your image at any point.
How much can I reduce the file size? ▾
It depends on the image and the quality setting you choose. For typical photographs, you can usually achieve a 60–80% reduction in file size at 80% quality with no visible difference. At lower quality settings, reductions of 90% or more are possible.
Can I compress a PNG without losing the transparent background? ▾
Yes. If you select PNG as the output format, the transparency in your image will be preserved. Note that JPG does not support transparency, so if you compress a PNG with a transparent background as a JPG, the transparent areas will become white.
What is the difference between JPG, PNG, and WEBP compression? ▾
JPG uses lossy compression, which means it permanently removes some image data to reduce file size. PNG uses lossless compression, which means no data is lost, but the file sizes are larger. WEBP supports both lossy and lossless compression and generally achieves smaller file sizes than both JPG and PNG at equivalent quality levels.
