JPG vs PNG vs WebP: Which Format Should You Actually Use?

A clear, practical breakdown of the three most common image formats — what makes them different, where each one wins, and how to pick the right one without guessing.

10 min read · Updated 2026

Every image on the internet is saved in some format, and most people never think about it until something goes wrong — a logo loses its transparent background, a photo balloons to 8MB, or a website loads painfully slowly. Almost all of these problems trace back to one thing: using the wrong image format for the job. This guide breaks down JPG, PNG, and WebP in plain language, so you can pick the right one on purpose instead of by accident.

The Short Answer, If You're in a Hurry

If that table answers your question, that's genuinely most of what you need to know. Keep reading for the reasoning behind it — understanding why makes the exceptions much easier to spot.

What Each Format Actually Is

JPG (JPEG) — The Default for Photos

JPG has been the standard photo format since the early 1990s, and it's still what most cameras and phones save to by default. It uses lossy compression, meaning it intelligently throws away small amounts of image data that the human eye is least likely to notice, in exchange for a much smaller file. That trade-off is why a JPG photo can be 5 to 10 times smaller than the same photo saved as PNG, usually without a visible quality difference at normal viewing size.

The catch: because JPG compression is lossy, quality degrades a little more every time you edit and re-save the same file. It also can't store transparency at all — every pixel in a JPG has a solid color, even the background.

PNG — The Standard for Graphics and Transparency

PNG was built as a lossless alternative to older formats, meaning it stores every single pixel exactly as it is, with nothing thrown away no matter how many times you save it. It also supports a full transparency channel, so parts of an image can be partially or fully see-through. This makes PNG the natural choice for logos, icons, screenshots, and anything with flat colors or sharp text, since JPG compression tends to blur those elements.

The trade-off is file size. Because nothing is compressed away, PNG files are typically much larger than an equivalent JPG, especially for photographic content with lots of color variation.

WebP — The Modern Format Built for the Web

WebP is a newer format developed by Google specifically to solve the JPG-vs-PNG trade-off. It supports both lossy and lossless compression in the same format, plus full transparency like PNG, but typically produces files 25–35% smaller than a comparable JPG and significantly smaller than an equivalent PNG. Nearly every modern browser supports it, which is why most websites have switched to serving WebP images automatically to speed up page loading.

The trade-off is compatibility outside the web. Some older software, certain design tools, and a handful of platforms still expect JPG or PNG specifically, so WebP isn't yet the safest choice for a file you're sending directly to someone rather than embedding on a webpage.

One-sentence summary of each: JPG trades quality for small size, PNG trades size for perfect quality and transparency, and WebP tries to get the best of both — small size and transparency — at the cost of slightly less universal support.

Lossy vs Lossless, Explained Simply

This single concept explains almost every practical difference between these formats. Lossy compression (used by JPG, and optionally by WebP) removes some image data permanently to shrink the file — a bit like summarizing a paragraph instead of quoting it word for word. Lossless compression (used by PNG, and optionally by WebP) repackages the data more efficiently without removing anything — like zipping a file, where unzipping it gives you back the exact original.

Lossy compression is great when the goal is a smaller file and a small amount of invisible detail loss is an acceptable trade. Lossless compression is essential when every pixel matters — for editing masters, logos, or anything where a slight blur or color shift would actually be noticeable.

Transparency: The Feature That Decides a Lot of Decisions

JPG has no concept of transparency whatsoever — every pixel must have a solid RGB color. PNG and WebP both support a full alpha channel, meaning any pixel can be anywhere from fully opaque to fully invisible. If your image needs to sit cleanly on top of another background — a logo on a webpage, an icon over a photo, a product cutout — it has to be PNG or WebP. There's no way around this with JPG; it's a hard technical limitation of the format, not a setting you can change.

File Size and Website Performance

For anyone running a website, file size isn't just a storage concern — it directly affects how fast pages load, and page speed is a real factor in both user experience and search engine rankings. Here's roughly how the three formats compare for a typical photographic image at the same visual quality:

FormatTypical relative sizeWhy
PNGLargest (baseline)Lossless — stores every pixel, no data discarded
JPG~70–90% smaller than PNGLossy — discards data the eye won't easily notice
WebP~25–35% smaller than JPGNewer compression algorithm, more efficient encoding

This is why most modern websites serve WebP by default where possible, and fall back to JPG or PNG only for compatibility with older browsers or specific tools that don't yet support WebP.

Quality: Where Each Format Actually Shows Its Weaknesses

Format choice matters most where the content plays to a format's specific weakness. JPG compression is tuned for busy, organic textures like skin, foliage, and sky — it hides its losses well there. It performs noticeably worse on flat colors, sharp edges, and text, often producing a soft "ringing" halo around letters and hard lines. This is exactly why screenshots and logos look cleaner as PNG: there's no lossy compression fighting against their sharp edges.

PNG has essentially no quality downside — the only cost is file size. WebP, when used in its lossy mode, shares some of JPG's weaknesses around very sharp edges, though its algorithm generally handles them better than JPG does at an equivalent file size.

A Common Myth: "Converting to PNG Improves Quality"

This one trips people up constantly. Converting a JPG to PNG does not restore any detail that JPG compression already discarded — that data is gone and can't be mathematically recovered. What conversion actually does is lock in the image exactly as it currently looks, in a lossless container, so nothing further is lost from future edits and re-saves. If your original JPG was blurry or heavily compressed, the PNG version will be exactly that blurry too, just protected from getting worse.

Practical Guide: Which Format for Which Situation

Photos and Camera Shots

Use JPG for general storage and sharing — it's universally supported and the file size stays manageable. If the photo is going on a website, serve it as WebP instead where possible; the visual difference at normal quality settings is negligible, but the load-time savings add up fast across a whole page.

Logos, Icons, and Graphics

Use PNG. The combination of transparency support and lossless sharp edges is exactly what logos and icons need, and file size is rarely a major concern for small graphic assets. For a website's logo specifically, WebP works too and saves some bandwidth, as long as your platform supports it.

Screenshots and Text-Heavy Images

Use PNG. JPG's compression noticeably blurs small text and hard UI edges, which matters a lot for tutorials, documentation, and anything where readability is the whole point.

Website Images in General

Default to WebP wherever your platform and audience support it — it's the best balance of quality and load speed available today. Keep JPG or PNG as a fallback for older browsers or for any tool in your workflow that doesn't yet handle WebP.

Printing

Use JPG for photographic prints, since it's what nearly every consumer and professional print service expects. Use PNG instead if the design needs a transparent background to sit on colored material, such as apparel or promotional products.

Archiving an Important Original

Use PNG (or a RAW format from your camera, if available) for anything you might want to edit extensively in the future. Keep the lossless master separate from the compressed JPG or WebP copies you actually share.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which format is best for website images? ▾
WebP is generally the best default for websites today, since it produces smaller files than JPG or PNG at similar visual quality, which speeds up page load. Fall back to JPG for photos or PNG for graphics/transparency if you need maximum compatibility with very old browsers or tools.
Is PNG always higher quality than JPG? ▾
PNG is lossless, so it never loses detail, while JPG's lossy compression can. But "higher quality" only matters if the JPG is compressed heavily. A lightly compressed JPG of a photo can look visually identical to a PNG while being a fraction of the file size.
Why is my PNG file so much bigger than a JPG of the same photo? ▾
PNG stores every pixel losslessly with no data thrown away, while JPG intentionally discards detail the eye won't easily notice. For photographic content, that trade-off usually makes PNG files 3 to 10 times larger than an equivalent JPG.
Does WebP support transparency like PNG? ▾
Yes. WebP supports a full alpha transparency channel just like PNG, while also offering both lossy and lossless compression modes, which is part of why it's replacing PNG in many web use cases.
Can all browsers and apps open WebP files? ▾
Nearly all modern browsers support WebP, but some older software, older versions of certain design tools, and some social platforms still expect JPG or PNG specifically. If you're sharing a file outside the web rather than embedding it on a page, JPG or PNG remains the safer universal choice.
Should I use JPG or PNG for a logo? ▾
Use PNG for logos. Logos usually need a transparent background and have sharp edges and flat colors, both of which PNG handles perfectly and JPG handles poorly, since JPG compression blurs sharp edges and cannot store transparency at all.
What happens if I convert a JPG to PNG — does it get better quality? ▾
No. Converting a JPG to PNG cannot restore detail that was already lost during JPG compression. It simply repackages the current image losslessly from that point forward, preventing further degradation on future saves, but it doesn't undo damage that's already there.
Which format should I use for printing photos? ▾
JPG is the standard format most print services and consumer printers expect for photographs. Use a high-quality, lightly compressed JPG for the best balance of print quality and manageable file size.
