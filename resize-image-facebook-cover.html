Learn the Basics

What Does It Mean to Resize an Image?

When you resize an image, you are changing the number of pixels it contains. Every digital image is made up of tiny square dots called pixels. The more pixels an image has, the larger and more detailed it is. When you reduce the number of pixels, the image becomes smaller. When you increase them, the image becomes larger.

For example, a photo taken on a modern smartphone might be 4000 × 3000 pixels. That is 12 million pixels in total. If you want to post that photo on a website, you might not need all those pixels. A size of 1200 × 900 pixels is usually more than enough for most websites, and the file will be much smaller and load faster.

On the other hand, if you are preparing an image for a large print poster, you might actually need to increase the size. In that case, our tool will scale up the image as much as possible, though keep in mind that making an image larger than its original size can sometimes make it look a little less sharp.

Pixel Forge Stack uses high-quality canvas rendering in your browser to make sure your resized images look as good as possible, whether you are scaling up or scaling down.

File Formats Explained

Which Image Format Should You Choose?

After resizing, you can save your image in three different formats. Each one has its own strengths, and choosing the right one depends on what you are going to use the image for. Here is a quick guide:

JPG / JPEG

Best for photos and images with lots of colours. Smallest file size. Ideal for websites, social media, and email.

PNG

Best when you need a transparent background. Also great for logos and text-heavy images where sharpness matters more than file size.

WEBP

The modern format. Smaller file size than JPG with better quality. Ideal for web use. Supported by all modern browsers.

If you are not sure, go with JPG for photos and PNG for graphics or logos. WEBP is the best choice if you are building a website and want fast load times.

Pro Tips

Tips for Getting the Best Results

Here are some simple tips that will help you get the best quality when resizing your images:

Always keep aspect ratio locked unless you specifically need a stretched image. Locking the ratio makes sure your image does not look distorted or squished.

Do not make images too much larger than the original. If you have a 500×500 image and you try to make it 5000×5000, it will look blurry. It is always better to start with a high-resolution photo.

Use WEBP for website images. WEBP files are smaller than JPG and PNG, which means your website will load faster. Faster websites rank higher on Google.

For social media, always use the correct platform size. If you upload an image that is the wrong size, social media platforms will crop or stretch it automatically, which usually looks bad. Use our presets to avoid this.

Quality at 80–92% is usually enough. You will not notice much difference between 92% quality and 100% quality in most images, but the file size at 92% is noticeably smaller. This is the sweet spot for most use cases.

Save the original image before resizing. Our tool processes the image in your browser and does not touch your original file, but it is still a good habit to keep a backup of your original high-resolution image.

Can I resize an image without losing quality? ▾
When you make an image smaller, you can keep the quality very high by setting the quality slider to 90% or above and saving as PNG.
